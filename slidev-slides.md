# The Shift

<div style="width: 180px; height: 2px; background: rgba(148,163,184,0.42); margin-top: 0.6rem;"></div>
<div style="margin-top: 1rem; max-width: 1120px;">
  <p style="margin: 0 0 0.65rem; font-size: 0.98rem; line-height: 1.26;">The issue is not that people suddenly changed.</p>
  <p style="margin: 0 0 1rem; font-size: 0.98rem; line-height: 1.26; font-weight: 600;">The terrain changed, but many institutions are still using old maps.</p>
  <div>
    <p style="margin: 0 0 0.45rem; font-size: 0.78rem; line-height: 1.22; color: rgba(226,232,240,0.76);">What changed:</p>
    <div style="margin: 0 0 1rem; font-size: 0.88rem; line-height: 1.3;">
      <div>Online systems reward visibility and responsiveness.</div>
      <div>Digital tools make feedback immediate.</div>
      <div>Networks let people coordinate directly.</div>
      <div>Platforms shape attention, identity, and expectations.</div>
    </div>
    <p style="margin: 0 0 0.45rem; font-size: 0.78rem; line-height: 1.22; color: rgba(226,232,240,0.76);">What did not change fast enough:</p>
    <div style="font-size: 0.88rem; line-height: 1.3;">
      <div>Schools.</div>
      <div>Workplaces.</div>
      <div>Healthcare systems.</div>
      <div>Bureaucratic authority.</div>
    </div>
  </div>
</div>

<!--
So the argument I’m making is not that people suddenly changed.

The environment changed.

Digital systems changed how people receive feedback,
how they coordinate with others,
and how they learn.

But institutions like schools, workplaces,
and professional training systems
still operate on assumptions built for an earlier environment.

This mismatch is what sociologists call cultural lag.
-->

# A Personal Example

<div style="width: 180px; height: 2px; background: rgba(148,163,184,0.42); margin-top: 0.6rem;"></div>
<div style="margin-top: 1rem; max-width: 1100px;">
  <p style="margin: 0 0 0.8rem; font-size: 0.96rem; line-height: 1.3;">During a gap year, I watched my mom help train AI systems using knowledge built over years of medical practice experience.</p>
  <p style="margin: 0 0 1rem; font-size: 0.96rem; line-height: 1.3;">At the same time, many of my peers were spending hundreds of hours studying for exams that those same systems are now being trained to perform.</p>
  <p style="margin: 0 0 0.35rem; font-size: 0.8rem; line-height: 1.22; color: rgba(226,232,240,0.76);">The sociological question:</p>
  <div style="font-size: 0.96rem; line-height: 1.32;">
    If technology changes how expertise is stored and distributed, how do institutions decide which forms of training and authority still count?
  </div>
</div>

<!--
During my gap year I saw something that stuck with me.

My mom is an OB-GYN with decades of experience.

She was helping train AI models using that experience.

At the same time,
many of my friends are pre-med students
spending years studying the reasoning
that those same systems are starting to perform.

That contrast raised a sociological question for me:

If technology changes how expertise is stored and distributed,
how do institutions decide which forms of training
and authority still count?
-->

# Why This Is Sociological

<div style="width: 180px; height: 2px; background: rgba(148,163,184,0.42); margin-top: 0.6rem;"></div>
<div style="margin-top: 1.35rem; max-width: 1120px;">

  <div style="margin-bottom: 1.1rem;">
    <div style="font-weight: 600; color: rgba(226,232,240,0.76); margin-bottom: 0.08rem;">Socialization</div>
    <div style="padding-left: 1.1rem;">
      Digital environments shape expectations about feedback, coordination, and identity.
    </div>
  </div>

  <div style="margin-bottom: 1.1rem;">
    <div style="font-weight: 600; color: rgba(226,232,240,0.76); margin-bottom: 0.08rem;">Cultural Lag</div>
    <div style="padding-left: 1.1rem;">
      Institutions adapt more slowly than the environments people are socialized in.
    </div>
  </div>

  <div>
    <div style="font-weight: 600; color: rgba(226,232,240,0.76); margin-bottom: 0.08rem;">Sociological Imagination</div>
    <div style="padding-left: 1.1rem;">
      A way of recognizing that what looks like personal failure may reflect the need for structural changes.
    </div>
  </div>

</div>

<!--
Sociology helps explain this through three ideas.

Socialization shapes expectations about how the world works.

Cultural lag describes how institutions change more slowly than the environments people are socialized in.

And the sociological imagination reminds us that what feels like a personal struggle may actually reflect a mismatch between individuals and institutions.
-->

The goal of this talk is not to reject institutions or celebrate technology.

Institutions still play an important role.

But sociology reminds us that legitimacy is not permanent.

It evolves with social conditions.

As technology reshapes how knowledge and coordination work,
institutions will need to rethink
what counts as expertise,
training,
and authority.

Otherwise new systems will emerge to fill the gap.
-->
