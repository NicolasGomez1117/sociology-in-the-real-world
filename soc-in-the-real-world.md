# Sociology in the Real World: Old Maps, New Terrain

My presentation focuses on a current social trend rather than a single article: the growing mismatch between digital life and the institutions that still organize education, work, and authority. A common explanation for why many younger people struggle in these settings is that they are distracted, disengaged, or insufficiently disciplined. My argument is that sociology gives us a broader way to interpret that situation. The issue is not simply that individuals changed. The social environment changed first, and many institutions are still responding with assumptions built for an earlier world.

Digital systems have changed how people receive feedback, learn, build identity, and coordinate with others. Online environments reward visibility, fast response, and direct connection. At the same time, major institutions such as schools, workplaces, and professional training systems often remain slower, more centralized, and more dependent on older forms of gatekeeping. This creates a gap between the way people are socialized and the way they are expected to perform once they enter formal institutions. In that sense, what looks like a personal problem can also be understood as a structural transition.

This topic is sociological because it connects directly to the sociological imagination and to cultural lag. The sociological imagination asks us to connect personal troubles to broader public issues. Cultural lag helps explain why institutions do not always adapt as quickly as technology and social expectations do. In this case, younger people were socialized in digital environments that shaped their expectations about trust, legitimacy, and coordination, while institutions are still trying to define authority using older models.

I use one personal example to make this more concrete. During a gap year, I watched my mother, an OB-GYN, help train AI systems using knowledge built over years of medical practice. At the same time, many of my peers were still spending years studying the kinds of reasoning these systems are increasingly being built to perform. That contrast raises a sociological question: if technology changes how expertise is stored, distributed, and applied, how do institutions decide what still counts as legitimate authority?

My conclusion is not that institutions should be discarded or that technology automatically improves society. The more useful question is how institutions and new forms of coordination can adapt to one another, rather than drifting further apart.
