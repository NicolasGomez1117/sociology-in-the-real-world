# Old Maps, New Terrain
## Technology, Legitimacy, and Institutional Lag in the Digital Age

SOC1010 · 5-minute discussion draft

---

# Opening Frame

> A lot of younger people are told they are distracted, ungrounded, or disengaged. But what if part of the issue is not personal failure? What if they were socialized in one kind of world and then asked to succeed in institutions built for another?

---

# The Core Analogy

> It is not just that people changed. The terrain changed, and many institutions are still using old maps.

- Online systems reward visibility and responsiveness
- Digital tools make feedback immediate
- Networks let people coordinate directly
- Institutions still rely on slower, more opaque authority

# Everyday Contrasts

- Online tools give immediate feedback; institutions often do not
- Digital spaces reward visible output; institutions often rely on hidden gatekeeping
- Apps personalize and adapt; bureaucracies standardize and delay
- Networks let people coordinate directly; institutions expect formal channels

# A Personal Example

During a gap year, I watched my mother, an OB-GYN, help train AI models using knowledge built over years of medical practice.

At the same time, students still spend years moving through older institutional pathways to gain access to that same knowledge.

## Sociological question

When technology changes how expertise is stored and distributed, how do institutions decide which forms of training, authority, and legitimacy still count?

---

# Not Just Centralization

Technology can concentrate power in platforms, but it also creates pressure toward distributed coordination.

- Remote care outside older facility-based models
- Small groups building locally using digital networks
- Online knowledge becoming real-world action

---

# Closing Claim

The goal is not to reject technology or romanticize institutions.

Technology has already reshaped how people imagine themselves, build trust, and organize action.

If institutions do not adapt, they may keep misreading structural change as personal failure.
